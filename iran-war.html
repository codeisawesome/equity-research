1. Executive Summary

Data-Backed Revision

The report's geopolitical logic still points to a persistent shock, but the macro state does not look like 1979. The local dataset shows current U.S. headline CPI at 2.39% on 2026-01-01, core CPI at 2.17%, and 10Y breakevens at 2.35% by 2026-03-06. That is materially tighter than 2019 on policy, but nowhere near the inflation regime of 1973-74 or 1979-80.

The closest macro analogue is 2019 Abqaiq, with 1990-91 Gulf War as the next-best stress template. In the dashboard data, current 2026 readings line up with Abqaiq on inflation, credit, and liquidity, while the post-shock equity and cyclical damage is starting to resemble Gulf War behavior more than a clean repair story.

The reason to stay in "persistent shock" is not the current U.S. macro print by itself. It is the combination of softer growth, restrictive policy, weaker earnings, and a transport-system shock that can last longer than the first market move. That makes this episode worse than Abqaiq on duration risk even if it does not yet look like a systemic macro break.

Revised Core View

Keep the report's base case, but narrow the framing. The macro evidence supports a 2019-style macro backdrop with a 1990-style drawdown risk and a more persistent logistics tail than either. That argues for lower rapid-normalization odds, slightly higher squeeze odds, and no move yet to a true systemic-break base case.

The original draft leaned heavily on structural oil-shock analogues. The local macro data says that is too strong for the current U.S. inflation backdrop, even if the logistics and regime story still justify persistence.

What Did Not Change

Hormuz impairment remains a transport bottleneck rather than a single-facility outage. That still argues against a simple Abqaiq-style V-shaped healing path.

Base Interpretation

Macro says Abqaiq or Gulf War. Politics and shipping say "stickier than Abqaiq." That is the cleanest way to reconcile the report with the data.

5. Probability Implications

Revision versus the original 20 / 50 / 20 / 10: lower rapid-normalization odds, slightly higher squeeze odds, lower systemic-break odds.

Rapid Normalization | 15%

Still possible if escorted shipping resumes and the market treats the event as a short-duration transport scare.

The local data argues against a larger weight because small caps and cyclicals are already slipping more than in a clean Abqaiq replay.

Persistent Shock | 55%

Best fit with the evidence. Macro state is not 1979, but restrictive policy, soft labor momentum, and the regime-hardening marker all support a slower unwind.

This is the scenario where equities stay heavy, XLE outperforms, and inflation expectations remain sticky without a true credit accident.

Severe Squeeze | 22%

This weight increases because current growth and earnings are weaker than in 2019, so the same oil shock can do more damage.

A further move in oil or broader Gulf infrastructure damage would push this higher quickly.

Systemic Break | 8%

Not zero, but current HY, IG, and NFCI readings do not support making crisis the base case.

The bar for moving this higher is real credit widening, funding stress, or an outright broader war.

Base / Bull / Bear Mapping

The dashboard's standing probabilities before the Iran overlay were 45.9% base, 28.5% bull, and 25.6% bear. The Iran revision argues for trimming bull and shifting that weight mostly into bear, not into a full crisis bucket.

6. Data Gaps / Method

Gap Why It Matters Current Handling
Point-in-time history before 2026 Older analogues are revised history, not true real-time macro snapshots. Used latest stored history and flagged the PIT limitation explicitly.
1973 / 1979 oil series coverage No local WTI or gasoline series for those analogues. Used inflation and growth behavior instead of inventing energy readings.
Credit history for early analogues No HY / IG OAS for 1973, 1979, or 1990 in the store. Relied on NFCI and bank-tightening coverage where available.
Modern sector ETF history No XLE / XLI / XLP / XLU history for 1973, 1979, or 1990. Used SP500 and RUT where available, and sector ETF reads only for 2019 and 2026.
Current macro window is incomplete Most monthly and quarterly data are pre-shock. Treated the current episode as a market-led early read rather than a fully confirmed macro regime shift.
External logistics metrics The local macro store does not contain Brent, tanker rates, or insurance premia. Kept the report's geopolitical logic, but did not pretend the macro database captures the full shipping channel.
Method

This revision used the local dashboard dataset rather than the unavailable local API endpoint. Data came from the SQLite store at macro-regime-dashboard/data/macro_data.sqlite3 and the dashboard's own transformation logic for feature metrics. Daily and weekly series were compared around T-5d, T0, T+5d, T+20d, and T+60d where history existed. Monthly and quarterly series used the last reading on or before the anchor date plus later releases where available.

The report source remained iran-war.html, but the analogue interpretation was revised to separate political persistence from starting macro regime. That is the central change in this version.

Bottom Line

The cleanest revision is not to abandon the original thesis, but to sharpen it: the macro backdrop looks like 2019 or 1990, while the logistics tail looks stickier than 2019. That is a persistent-shock call, not a 1979 replay and not a systemic-break base case.
