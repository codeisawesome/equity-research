1. Executive Summary

Normalized Draft

The cleanest way to frame this is not "Middle East risk premium." It is a live transport-system shock layered on top of regime hardening. The war began on February 28, 2026, when U.S. and Israeli strikes killed Ali Khamenei; by March 8, Iran's Assembly of Experts had elevated Mojtaba Khamenei, which materially lowers the odds of a quick political climbdown.

The macro hinge is the Strait of Hormuz. Official closure or not, commercial reality already looks broken. Reuters reported shipping had ground to a near halt within days of the opening strikes, and AP reported on March 9 that tanker traffic had been all but stopped by missile and drone risk. That matters more than any single damaged facility because Hormuz is a system bottleneck, not a repair job.

The conflict is also no longer bilateral. Reuters confirmed a drone strike on RAF Akrotiri in Cyprus, and AP confirmed Bahraini officials accused Iran of damaging a desalination plant. That combination matters because it widens the war's footprint from oil and missiles into allied basing, water infrastructure, insurance markets, and Gulf-state domestic stability.

My base case remains "persistent shock," not "quick normalization." Brent opened around $107.97 on March 9 according to AP, already above the psychological threshold that starts feeding inflation expectations back into equities and rates. Even if the shooting slows, war-risk insurance, fleet positioning, and owner caution can keep flows impaired after headlines cool down.

Inference: Mojtaba's succession, the White House's maximalist rhetoric, and the shift from facility damage to transit paralysis together argue for a slower market healing process than the first-pass bullish reflex usually assumes.

Core View

This report has been rewritten around confirmed facts. The earlier draft leaned too hard on a few specific numbers that were difficult to verify cleanly. The durable thesis survives: Hormuz impairment plus hardline succession is more consistent with a sticky oil shock than with a quick V-shaped reset.

Pillar 1: Hardline Succession

Mojtaba Khamenei's elevation points to continuity under stress, not capitulation. That does not guarantee escalation, but it clearly weakens the case for a fast diplomatic off-ramp.

Pillar 2: Chokepoint Damage Beats Facility Damage

Abqaiq-style outages can be repaired. Hormuz disruption is a traffic, insurance, routing, and confidence problem spread across multiple countries and fleets.

Cyprus, Bahrain, Gulf desalination assets, and Lebanese fronts are now part of the same market story. The war's perimeter has widened faster than most asset prices initially assumed.

2. Market Structure

The Critical Macro Fact

The most important edit to the original draft is semantic but meaningful: Hormuz is not best described as formally "closed." It is better described as commercially impaired to the point of near-freeze. That wording matches the reporting more closely and still captures the market consequence.

Reuters reported on March 2 that shipping through the strait had ground to a near halt and that about 150 ships were stranded around Hormuz as insurers pulled war-risk cover. By March 6, shipping monitors were reporting only two ships had passed in the prior 24 hours, neither of them tankers. AP then reported on March 9 that tanker movement had effectively stopped under missile and drone threat.

Why This Matters
  • Hormuz normally carries roughly one-fifth of global oil flows.
  • It also handles key LNG exports, especially from Qatar.
  • When shipping stops, producers cannot simply "wait it out" because storage fills quickly.
  • That turns a maritime-security event into an upstream production problem.
Why This Is Worse Than A Single Facility Outage
  • A refinery or processing plant can be inspected, repaired, and restarted.
  • A transit corridor requires military deterrence, insurer confidence, and shipowner willingness.
  • Even after a ceasefire, shipping economics can remain impaired.
  • That is why the shock can outlive the military headlines.

3. Historical Analogues

Framework

Past oil shocks usually fit one of two buckets. The first is the sharp but reversible spike: violent price action, heavy headlines, then relatively quick normalization once the damaged asset or threatened route is brought back under control. The 1990-91 Gulf War and the 2019 Abqaiq strike are the most useful reference points there.

The second is the structural shock: revolution, sanctions, civil conflict, or state breakdown that keeps supply impaired well after the first military phase ends. The 1973-74 embargo and the 1979-80 Iranian revolution are the cleaner templates.

The current episode looks like a hybrid but leans toward the second bucket. The succession outcome hardens the political side of the story, while Hormuz disruption hardens the logistics side. That combination is what makes this episode more dangerous than a generic "risk premium" move.

1990-91 / 2019 Template

Pattern: fast spike, then repair and rerouting.

Bull case for today: convoy protection works, insurers return, and Gulf exports resume faster than feared.

1979-80 Template

Pattern: political break plus extended supply disruption.

Bear case for today: hardline succession reduces the odds of compromise while transit risk stays elevated.

2026 Working Analogue

Pattern: initial military shock, then a slower unwind driven by insurance, shipping, and regime posture.

My read: closer to a persistent chokepoint event than to a quick repair story.

Key Distinction From Abqaiq

The draft's strongest idea was also the right one: Abqaiq was a facility outage. Hormuz is a transit-system outage. That distinction is worth keeping because it changes the expected duration profile. Even if missiles stop, the market still needs insurers, charterers, crews, and naval protection to believe the route is usable again.

Rapid Off-Ramp | 20%

Brent retraces toward roughly $85-$95, escorted traffic resumes, and risk assets stabilize.

Needs both a political off-ramp and credible Hormuz reopening.

Persistent Shock | 50%

Brent holds around $100-$125, inflation expectations stay sticky, and the S&P 500 remains roughly 5% to 12% below its pre-shock range.

This is the most plausible path if the war continues without a full U.S. ground campaign but shipping remains impaired.

Severe Squeeze | 20%

Brent moves into the $125-$150 band, jet fuel and diesel stress intensify, and cyclicals break harder.

Requires continued traffic paralysis plus further damage to Gulf infrastructure.

Systemic Break | 10%

Brent pushes beyond $150, policy response turns emergency-oriented, and markets enter full panic conditions.

Likely requires multi-country production losses or overt expansion of the war.

Bottom Line

This report should be read as a market note, not as a battlefield forecast. The investable point is that the shock has already migrated from headline risk to logistics impairment. Once that happens, the bar for a clean reset rises sharply. The original draft was directionally right on that point, but it is stronger after removing a few over-specific claims and anchoring the thesis to what is actually confirmed.
